A11y as the Rule, Not the Exception

28 December 2025

  • #cpacc
  • #basics

Disabilities are more common than you think. Roughly 1 in 5 people has a disability of some kind. Sometimes a person's disability is immediately obvious, like blindness or someone in a wheelchair. Other times, it may not be as easily recognizable. How would you know that someone you pass on the street has a reading disorder? You probably wouldn't until a situation arose that revealed it.

There's a good chance that we'll all experience a disability at some point in our lives. Sometimes disabilities are temporary, like when someone breaks an arm or a recent surgery prevents movement. Disabilities are also age-dependent. Our sight, hearing, cognition and mobility are all affected by the aging process. As we get older, or abilities diminish. This is a normal part of what it means to be a human.

But not all disabilities affect a person's ability to use the web. As web developers, we are concerned with disabilities that affect a persons sight, hearing, and cognition.

A11y Matters

Printed materials are everywhere - from product packaging, business cards, postal mail, and signs. Anyone with a visual disability will not be able to read these materials. Historically, blind people relied on others to read to them, record information, or create braille copies. But these options can be limited to having someone willing and able to read or record information for you. Screen readers and digital text offer a more independent way for blind people to access information, but only when digital resources are accessible in the first place.

Assistive Technologies

Assistive technologies are used by people to help compensate for a lack in ability in certain areas. Typically when people think about assistive technology, many think about wheelchairs, hearing aids, and canes. However, glasses and contacts are another type of common assistive technology - often used by people who don't consider themselves to be disabled.

Some assistive technologies invented to help those with disablilties access the web are:

  • screen readers
  • refreshable braille devices
  • screen enlargers
  • color enhancement overlays
  • captions and transcripts
  • head wands or mouth sticks
  • eye gaze tracking
  • voice activation

Assistive technologies are helpful, but they don't solve every obstacle a person might encounter. This is especially true if the person creating the content does not create them with accessibility in mind. It is up to us as developers to put accessibility at the forefront.
