Blindness

Blindness can be defined as sightlessness or loss of vision. However, the tern blindness doesn't always refer to a loss of vision. It may also refer to partial blindness, or when a persons vision is extremely limited.

Causes of Blindness

There are several causes of blindness (not limited to):

  • Diabetes
  • Macular Degeneration
  • Glaucoma
  • Accidents or injury to the eye
  • Stroke
  • Retinitis Pigmentosa

Legal Definition of Blindness

In the US, there is a legal definition of blindness that is used to determine whether someone can receive benefits and participate in certain programs for schooling or vocational training, assistive devices, and tax exemption. This definition isn't intended to provide information about what a person can or can not see. According to this definition, a person is considered legally blind if they have a visual acuity of 20/200 with corrective lenses or if their field of vision is 20 degrees in the eye that has the best vision.

Challenges & Solutions for People Who Are Blind

Blind people use screen readers to get information from the web. Screen readers are software that are designed to turn text into speech. Blind people listen to the web.

Digital Environments
Challenge Solutions
Cannot see digital interfaces
  • screen readers
  • self-voicing interfaces
  • refreshable braille
Content and interfaces are not accessible Developers and designers can use sematic HTML and universal design techniques to make websites compatible with assistive technologies.
Physical Environments
Challenge Solutions
Cannot see when walking
  • a cane to "feel" their surroundings
  • service animals
  • GPS-based walking instructions with audio
  • raised tiles on edges of stairs, platforms, pathways, and sidewalks
  • remove low-hanging architecture that people can bump into
  • keep pathways clear
Cannot see signs or text on buildings
  • tactile maps to help people form a metal map
  • map and geolocation apps on mobile devices
  • braille labels on entrances, bathrooms, rooms, markers and POIs
  • raised tiles on edges of stairs, platforms, pathways, and sidewalks
  • AI tools can provide descriptions of text, people, currency, and other objects
Consumer Products
Challenge Solutions
Flat surfaces on microwaves, ovens, and dishwashers
  • alternative interfaces with knobs or tactile controls
  • audio interfaces
  • remote control through apps on mobile devices
Cannot read text on packaging (toothpaste, shampoo, cleaners, food, drinks) embossed braille on products helps consumers in-store and at home
Cannot read money to determine its value
  • apps can identify the value through photographs
  • paper bills and coins can have different sizes, shapes or textures to help distinguish its value
  • cashless payment systems via computers, mobile devices, or in-store hardware that has voice output and screen readers
Cannot read printed materials
  • optical character recognition software
  • conversion to digital format
  • conversion to braille
